Pollination

How Bees Pollinate

Bees are some of the most efficient pollinators in the world. As they travel from flower to flower collecting nectar, pollen from the flowers' anthers sticks to their fuzzy bodies. When they land on another flower, some of this pollen is transferred to the stigma, allowing plants to reproduce. This process of pollination is crucial for the growth of many crops and wild plants.

Approximately 75% of the world's food crops depend on pollination to some extent. Without bees, many fruits, vegetables, and nuts would become scarce and expensive. Bees are essential to maintaining biodiversity and ecosystems, as they help plants reproduce, providing food and habitats for other animals.

Medicinal Use

Medicinal Products Produced by Bees

In addition to their role as pollinators, bees produce several substances with medicinal properties. Honey has natural antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties, making it effective in wound healing. Bees also produce propolis, a resin-like material with antiseptic and antioxidant properties, and royal jelly, which is believed to boost the immune system and improve skin health.
